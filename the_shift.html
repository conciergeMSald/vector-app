I
1 in 5

By 2030, one in five Americans will be over 65. The careers built to serve them — in medicine, housing, technology, finance, and law — don't have names yet. The students who will build those careers are in high school right now.

II
$84 trillion

The largest transfer of wealth in human history is happening right now. Baby boomers are passing $84 trillion to their heirs. Most of the people who will manage that transfer — in wealth management, estate law, family office, and philanthropic advising — are sitting in a classroom, and no one has told them yet.

III
The new frontier

The fastest-growing companies in home services, dental, veterinary care, and HVAC are not startups. They are private equity firms buying family businesses and professionalizing industries that have never been professionalized. The people who will run those platforms are in college right now. Nobody has pointed at them yet.

IV
The map
is moving

Austin. Nashville. Phoenix. Raleigh. Tampa. The American population is redistributing itself in real time. The careers that follow — in real estate, infrastructure, urban planning, and commercial development — are concentrated in corridors that didn't exist as career destinations a decade ago. Where you go to school determines whether you see this coming.

V
The builder
returns

The student who grows up watching their father build houses does not need to become their father. They need to become what their father could not become because no one showed them the door. Construction management. Real estate development. PropTech. The college-educated builder is one of the most valuable and most overlooked profiles in the American economy.

VI
The spine

Middle management is not a lesser thing. The operations manager who runs a 200-person regional team, the quality control director who saves a manufacturer $40 million, the customer experience leader who turns around a brand — these are the people who make everything else work. They are well-compensated, in demand, and completely invisible in college planning. Until now.

VII
They are
16

The workforce that built the last 40 years is retiring. The workforce that will build the next 40 is in high school. They will inherit an economy that is older, wealthier, more geographically distributed, and more structurally complex than anything that has come before it. Someone should tell them what they're walking into.
