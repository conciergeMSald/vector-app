I
1 in 5

By 2030, one in five Americans will be over 65. The careers built to serve them — in medicine, housing, technology, finance, and law — don't have names yet. The students who will build those careers are in high school right now.

II
$84 trillion

The largest transfer of wealth in human history is happening right now. Baby boomers are passing $84 trillion to their heirs. Most of the people who will manage that transfer — in wealth management, estate law, family office, and philanthropic advising — are sitting in a classroom, and no one has told them yet.

III
The coming
industries

Private equity is buying dental practices, veterinary clinics, HVAC companies, and home services businesses and professionalizing them at scale. Nutraceuticals and longevity medicine are becoming the consumer health economy of the next decade. Defense technology, cybersecurity, climate adaptation infrastructure, and AI operations are generating demand for talent that no university has named yet. The people who will lead these industries are in college right now. The industries don't have names in the course catalog. They are being built in real time.

IV
The map
is moving

Austin. Nashville. Phoenix. Raleigh. Tampa. The American population is redistributing itself in real time. The careers that follow — in real estate, infrastructure, urban planning, and commercial development — are concentrated in corridors that didn't exist as career destinations a decade ago. Where you go to school determines whether you see this coming.

V
The inheritance

The rising generation does not need to leave what was built. They need to take it further than the generation that built it could. Construction management. Real estate development. The family enterprise elevated by someone who was shown the door that their parents couldn't find. The college-educated operator who steps into a family business and transforms it is one of the most valuable and most overlooked profiles in the American economy. They inherit something real. They make it more.

VI
The spine

Remove the spine and nothing stands. The surgeon gets credit. The hospital administrator who ensured the operating room was staffed, the supply chain was intact, and the insurance was processed does not. The operations manager who runs a 200-person regional team, the quality control director who saves a manufacturer $40 million, the customer experience leader who turns around a brand — these are the people who make everything else work. They are well-compensated, in demand, and completely invisible in college planning. The spine is not a lesser thing. It is the reason the body can stand at all.

VII
Today,
they are 16

The workforce that built the last 40 years is retiring. The seats are emptying in every industry simultaneously. The workforce that will fill them is in high school right now — and they are walking into the largest transfer of professional opportunity in modern history. There is a path that almost no college counselor mentions: the military's non-combat civilian infrastructure roles — logistics, cybersecurity, healthcare administration, intelligence analysis, communications, engineering — place highly capable young people into some of the most rigorous operational environments in the world. A 22-year-old who completes that path enters civilian life at 26 with a security clearance, operational experience, and a professional network that no MBA program produces at that age. The demographics are clear. The seats are there. The question is who knows where to walk. VECTOR was built to answer that.
